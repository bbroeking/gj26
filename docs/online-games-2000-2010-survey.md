# Popular Online Games 2000–2010 — Survey & Design Lessons

A condensed survey of the era that shaped MMOs, browser games, and the genre conventions modern games still rely on. Catalogued by category, with one-line "why it was fun" notes per title and a synthesis at the end.

The 2000s were the **golden age of social online games** — broadband became universal, F2P invented, the genre vocabulary locked in. Many design ideas modern games quietly inherit (skill grinds, daily cooldowns, persistent worlds, gift-heart systems) crystallized here.

---

## 1. The era's defining traits

Five things made 2000–2010 special and underrated by today's design discourse:

| Trait | What it meant | Why it mattered |
|---|---|---|
| **Browser-first or low-spec** | Java, Flash, custom engines that ran on any potato | Removed the install barrier; opened gaming to non-PC homes |
| **Social-heavy** | Chat rooms, guilds, in-world hangouts as core, not optional | Players bonded with strangers — friendships outlasted the games |
| **Community-driven** | Forums, fan wikis, fansites, fan-art, fan-tools | The metagame *outside* the game was half the experience |
| **Persistent worlds** | World ticks even when you log off | Created stakes (raids, decay) and FOMO without exploitation |
| **Slow progression** | Levels and skills took weeks/months | Made a 99 *mean* something. Compare modern instant-gratification curves |

## 2. Survey by category

### 2.1 The big MMORPGs

| Game | Year | Why it was fun | Design lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ultima Online** | 1997 | Sandbox economy, free-form roleplay, player-built houses with key persistence, full-loot PvP. Felt like a *world* before "MMO" was a word. | Sandbox > themepark for emergent stories. But sandbox needs structure or it collapses. |
| **EverQuest** | 1999 | The first 3D MMO that nailed group-forced PvE — you literally couldn't level without a party. Forged friendships under pressure. | Forced grouping = social glue, but it's a trap for solo players. Modern descendants made it optional and lost the magic. |
| **Phantasy Star Online** | 2000 | Console MMO with lobby-based dungeons, partner cards, drop-in coop. *Charmingly* sci-fi. | Instanced co-op shipped *before* WoW dungeons. Lobby + match was a brilliant simplification. |
| **RuneScape** | 2001 | Browser-based, F2P, 23 distinct skills, every action gives XP. Walk anywhere, do anything, freedom over depth. | **Multiple parallel progression vectors** is the cheat code for retention. When one stalls, another moves. |
| **Final Fantasy XI** | 2002 | JRPG-meets-MMO. Brutal corpse runs, Linkshells (unique social structure), jaw-dropping CG cutscenes. | Difficulty + scarcity = community. Modern XIV inherits the soul. |
| **Ragnarok Online** | 2002 | Anime aesthetic, 2D sprites on 3D world, party-buff dependency, MVPs (giant boss spawns). Korean grind tradition. | Class identity through buff/debuff dependencies = forced social. |
| **Lineage II** | 2003 | Castle sieges, clan wars, harsh PvP, deep economy. The Korean MMO that introduced massive PvP. | Persistent territory control creates 24/7 player drama better than any quest. |
| **EVE Online** | 2003 | One server, real economy, real wars (THE B-R5RB battle cost ~$300K USD in player assets), spreadsheets in space. | Single-shard + player-driven everything = newspaper-worthy emergent stories. Hardcore by design. |
| **Final Fantasy XI**, again | 2002 | (see above) | — |
| **World of Warcraft** | 2004 | Streamlined every EverQuest pain point. Quest-driven leveling. Solo-friendly until endgame. Brilliant onboarding. | **Streamline what was painful, keep what was meaningful.** The single most important design playbook of the era. |
| **City of Heroes** | 2004 | Costume creator was *the feature*. Mission architect let players build content. Superhero RP server thrived. | Player-generated content + identity expression = lifetime customers. Devs killed it; players resurrected it 10 years later. |
| **EverQuest II** | 2004 | More polished EQ; eclipsed by WoW launching same month. | Timing matters more than quality. |
| **Guild Wars** | 2005 | Instanced PvE, no monthly fee, "skill > grind" — weekly tournaments with cash prizes. | F2P with cosmetic-only monetization beat subscription for many. Pre-pioneered what GW2 / LoL do today. |
| **MapleStory** | 2003 (KR) / 2005 (NA) | 2D side-scrolling, F2P with cash shop, anime cute, brutal grind. The first F2P MMO to hit Western teens. | F2P + cash cosmetics worked. Beat subscriptions to the West by years. |
| **LotRO** | 2007 | Tolkien IP done with reverence. Music system (real player concerts). The "Premium" model. | Genuine IP love beats half-hearted IP love. Players noticed. |
| **Wizard101** | 2008 | Card-based combat, school theme, parental-friendly chat. Kid MMORPG done right. | Turn-based combat + cards = lower input load = wider audience. |
| **Dofus / Wakfu** | 2004 / 2012 | Tactical-grid combat, French art style, deep economy, profession-driven crafting. | Tactical combat in an MMO works. Most games copied WoW; Dofus copied Final Fantasy Tactics and won a niche. |
| **Aion** | 2009 | Beautiful art, faction PvP (Abyss), wing flight as core. NCSoft's WoW-killer that wasn't. | Vertical movement + faction PvP looked great, didn't sustain. Beauty isn't enough. |

### 2.2 Browser & social — the kids who became gamers

| Game | Year | Why it was fun | Design lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neopets** | 1999 | Pet-care + gold + auctions + battles + dungeons + writing contests. Felt like 30 games in one. | Sprawling sub-systems with a cute frame can hide infinite content. Vivendi nearly ruined it; nostalgia keeps it alive. |
| **Habbo Hotel** | 2000 | Pixel-art rooms you decorated and hung out in. Furni economy, pure social. | **The hangout *is* the game.** No goals, just vibes. Vast revenue from cosmetic furni. |
| **AdventureQuest** | 2002 | Single-player flash RPG with weekly content drops. Devs were *part of the community*. | Episodic small content beats one giant patch. Community access to devs = loyalty. |
| **Toontown Online** | 2003 | Disney's MMO — co-op gag-throwing combat against cog suits. Family-friendly, queue-based safety chat. | Pre-canned chat + open chat = age-bracket-appropriate gameplay. |
| **Puzzle Pirates** | 2003 | Skill-based puzzle minigames replaced combat (Bejeweled-as-cannon-firing). Player-run economy and pirate crews. | Puzzle-as-skill-system works. No twitch, all pattern recognition. |
| **Kingdom of Loathing** | 2003 | Stick-figure HTML/text game, free, pun-based combat, no graphics, brilliant writing. Still active 22 years later. | **Writing > graphics**. Free + smart = perpetual lifetime. |
| **Gaia Online** | 2003 | Anime-style avatar dress-up + forums + minigames. Cosmetic obsession. | Forums-as-MMO. Identity through avatar. |
| **Second Life** | 2003 | Adult version of Habbo. Real currency exchange. Players built and *sold* anything — clothes, scripts, scenes. | Real-economy virtual worlds work but require real legal/regulatory infrastructure. |
| **Stardoll** | 2004 | Dress-up dolls + paperdoll celebs + cash-shop fashion. 400M users at peak. | Underestimated by gaming press; massive girl-audience MMO. |
| **Club Penguin** | 2005 | Penguin avatars + minigames + parties + igloos + safe chat. Disney bought it for $700M. | Safe-chat moderation + holiday events = parent-approved subscription gold mine. |
| **Webkinz** | 2005 | Plush toy IRL + virtual code → online pet. Cross-medium loop. | Physical-to-digital tie-ins worked when execution was clean. Most clones failed. |
| **Moshi Monsters** | 2008 | Pet monster collecting + puzzles. UK-led; 80M users at peak. | British charm + pet care = same loop as Tamagotchi/Neopets, fresh skin. |
| **Free Realms** | 2009 | Sony's free F2P kids MMO. Mini-games (kart racing!), pet system, build-your-own outfit. SOE killed it. | Even good kid MMOs die when publishers redirect attention. RIP. |

### 2.3 Browser strategy / persistent

| Game | Year | Why it was fun | Design lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OGame** | 2002 | Space-empire builder. Resources tick in real-time even logged out. Raids cost others fleet → drama. | **Persistence creates stakes**. Logging off = vulnerability = engagement. |
| **Tribal Wars** | 2003 | Medieval village builder. Tribe alliances. Build → research → war → night-attack timing. | Real-time persistence + sleep windows = emergent timezone politics. |
| **Travian** | 2004 | Same but more polished. Servers reset every 200 days → fresh start, "win the server." | **Server-reset cadence solves the new-player problem**. Everyone starts fresh on a date. |
| **Ikariam** | 2008 | Greek-themed Travian-like. More PvE-friendly. | Polish + accessibility variant of the same template. |
| **Grepolis** | 2009 | Travian + Greek mythology + heroes. The "feature creep" version. | Genre saturation by 2009. Still profitable; not innovative. |

### 2.4 Lobby / matchmade multiplayer (counts because online + multiplayer)

| Game | Year | Why it was fun | Design lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Counter-Strike 1.6** | 2000 (mod 1999) | Best-in-class 5v5 round-based shooter. Servers had personality, admins had power. | Player-run servers = community ownership. Modern matchmaking lost this. |
| **Diablo II + Battle.net** | 2000 | Loot grind + ladder seasons + trade economy. Spawned the entire ARPG genre. | **Ladder seasons** were invented here. Now standard in every live-service game. |
| **StarCraft / WC3 / DotA** | 2002–2003 | Custom maps as a feature → mods became standalone games (DotA → MOBA genre). | **Mod-friendly engines breed genres.** TF2, DotA, CS, Garry's Mod all came from this idea. |
| **Halo 2 (Xbox Live)** | 2004 | First console online matchmaking that felt seamless. | Matchmaking as UX innovation. Players don't want lobbies they want games. |
| **Battlefield 1942 / 2** | 2002 / 2005 | 32–64 player vehicle combat, large maps. The "big sandbox shooter." | Scale + vehicles + classes = unique fun no other genre captured. |

## 3. Cross-cutting patterns — the ingredients

Recurring traits in the games that lasted:

### A. Multiple progression vectors
RuneScape, EQ, EVE all had **many parallel curves** (skills, levels, gear, reputation, professions). When one stalls, another moves. WoW added alts as a vector — every retention strategy modern games use is in this template.

### B. Persistent world ticks
OGame, Travian, EVE, Tribal Wars all had the world running while you slept. Created urgency, vulnerability, and 24/7 drama without forcing players to stay logged in.

### C. The grind that meant something
99 Woodcutting in RuneScape took *real* months. A max-level WoW character felt earned. Modern games rush this; players notice and don't bond as deeply.

### D. Built-in social glue
Forced grouping (EQ), trade economies (RS, EVE), guild houses (UO), in-world hangouts (Habbo). Players couldn't avoid each other — and the friendships outlasted the games.

### E. Community-as-content
Player wikis, fan tools, YouTube essays, fanfic. The discourse outside the game was *part* of the game. Devs who engaged with the community (AdventureQuest, OSRS polling) won loyalty. Devs who ignored it (Wildstar) didn't.

### F. Free-to-play with cosmetic monetization
RuneScape, MapleStory, Guild Wars, Habbo, Stardoll — F2P + paid cosmetics dominated. Subscription games (WoW) are the *exception* historically. Modern industry forgot this and reinvented gacha.

### G. Server-reset / season cadence
Travian's 200-day reset, Diablo 2 ladder, OSRS leagues, EVE's deployment cycles. The fresh-start moment brings lapsed players back. **Most underused mechanic in modern MMOs.**

### H. Identity through character expression
City of Heroes' costume creator, Habbo's furni, Gaia avatars, MapleStory cash-shop cosmetics. Players paid real money to *be someone* in a world. The character creator was the product.

### I. Slow, generous tutorials
Tutorial Island (RS), Camp Verdant Hills (UO renaissance), the WoW starting zones. Each was patient and let you *try* before *succeed*. Modern games rush this and lose retention.

### J. Live events
Halloween events, Christmas trees in town squares, Easter egg hunts. Tied players to the calendar. Made absent players FOMO.

## 4. What we lost (and should bring back)

The 2010s flattened a lot of the era's magic. Worth re-evaluating for any cozy/MMO-flavored project:

1. **Player-run servers / community moderation.** CS, UO shards, EQ progression realms. Modern matchmaking + corporate moderation removed culture.
2. **Slow grinds with weight.** Modern games' "respect player time" is good, but went too far — instant unlocks have no emotional weight.
3. **Forced grouping (in moderation).** Solo MMO playerbases churn. EQ-style group dependency built friendships modern games can't.
4. **Mod-friendly engines.** Source/Bethesda's modding eras created genres. Modern engines lock down content.
5. **Custom-map / private-game lobbies.** WC3 custom maps → DotA → MOBA. Today's games skip this.
6. **Episodic small content.** AdventureQuest's weekly drops vs WoW's giant quarterly patches. Small-and-frequent beat big-and-rare for retention.
7. **Cosmetic-only monetization.** No P2W, just looking good. Underused in indie now that gacha rules.
8. **Real holiday/world events.** Tied to real calendar. Most modern games skip them or make them boring loot grinds.

## 5. Lessons for gj26 specifically

The cozy RuneScape × Harvest Moon hybrid you're building lands squarely in this era's DNA. Concrete picks:

| Lesson | gj26 application |
|---|---|
| **Multiple progression vectors** | 4 skills + hearts + cosmetics + house-decoration (post-jam) — already in DESIGN_VISION §5 |
| **Persistent world ticks** | Day/night cycles, NPC schedules, crop growth — already planned |
| **Grind that means something** | Skill-99 grind feels *real* because of generous-but-not-instant XP curves. Don't shortcut |
| **Built-in social glue (post-multiplayer)** | When multiplayer ships, force a town hub where players can't avoid each other. Don't instance everything |
| **Community-as-content** | Build appearance-string export from day 1 (tracked in `character-creator-research.md` §5). Players will share characters |
| **F2P + cosmetic monetization** | If you ever monetize: cosmetic dyes, decorations, NEVER stat-buying. RS/MapleStory/Habbo all proved this works |
| **Server-reset cadence** | Post-multiplayer: consider seasonal "fresh meadow" servers. Solves new-player problem |
| **Slow generous tutorial** | Already in `ONBOARDING.md` — Tutorial Island pattern, one NPC per system |
| **Live events** | Festival on day 14 (already planned). Add Halloween/Winter equivalents post-launch |
| **Charm in writing** | KoL proved that wit + free + zero graphics > big budget + bland. Your IM Fell English / hand-drawn look is already chasing this. *Hire/be a writer.* Bad text destroys cozy faster than bad graphics |

## 6. The "spirit of the era" smoke test for gj26

If a design decision passes none of these, reconsider:

- [ ] Does it create a moment a player would *tell their friend* about?
- [ ] Could a 12-year-old in 2007, on a family PC, have fallen in love with this?
- [ ] Does it reward patience without punishing missed days?
- [ ] Is there a community-content angle (sharing, modding, fan-art-ing)?
- [ ] Will I *feel* something when I hit 99 Woodcutting (or its equivalent)?
- [ ] Can I see other players being themselves in the world?
- [ ] Is there a calendar event I'd be sad to miss?
