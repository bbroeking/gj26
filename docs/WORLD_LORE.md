# Bramblewood — World Lore

**Status:** companion to `WORLD_BIBLE.md`. Use this for codex copy, journal lines, and any place text-flavor ships to the player. Anti-patterns from the Bible (no multi-page mythology, no save-the-world stakes, no grim flips) still apply — every entry below is **suggestion**, not pronouncement.

---

## Founding (one paragraph, plain)

Bramblewood was a hedge-trimmer's hamlet before it was a village. The first families came up from Sallow's End three or four winters after the tide receded — hedge-cutters and a wool-comber and one widowed cooper. They cleared a clearing in the old enclosure, built nine cottages, dug Old Mother Well, and began trimming the hedge instead of fighting it. The trimming has continued every spring since.

> *"We don't tame the brambles. We tend them. There's a difference."* — Maud Pennycress

## The Quickening

A week each spring when the bramble-imps grow bolder. They steal pies, untie washing lines, agitate the cows. Older villagers say it's the hedge "stretching," like a sleeper rolling over. Eldra burns extra wicks. Pell rings the cloister bell at sundown for seven nights running. After the seventh night, the imps quiet again. Most player combat happens during a Quickening — or close enough to one that no one minds the difference.

## The Hedgemother (the boss, lightly explained)

A long-ago hedge-witch named Onywyn — yes, the same name — bound herself into the bramble during the *Hard Frost of the Long Winter* to keep the village from freezing. She is not malevolent. She is not asleep. She is *thinking very slowly*, and when she thinks too hard the bramble wakes. The current Mother Onywyn is her great-granddaughter, who knows this and does not speak of it.

> *"Some kindnesses are too big to carry. So we plant them, and let the kindness be the place."* — Mother Onywyn

The Hedgemother boss fight is the player approaching that buried kindness. Defeating her is not killing her — it's *quieting her thinking* for a season, which is enough.

## Two old soldiers (faction undercurrent — gentle)

**Hod Tenter** and **Sir Withering of Trelliswick** are both veterans of wars they don't discuss. The wars were not the same war.

- Withering fought a *named* war — under banners, with knights, for a duke whose name still appears in old ledgers. He talks about it the way you talk about a school you didn't enjoy.
- Hod fought a *forgotten* war — small, ugly, between hedgerows. He was probably on the wrong side. He has not used a weapon in twenty years, only hammers.

The two men are friends in the way that two old soldiers can be without ever telling each other anything. They drink at the well on Pieday and don't speak. The village finds this comforting.

## The two herb-tenders (faction undercurrent — sharp but small)

**Brother Pell** and **Mother Onywyn** disagree about herbs.

- Pell's cloister keeps a small *index* — the *Marked Pages* — listing which herbs are safe, which are reagent-only, which are *not for use*. The list has been added to slowly for two centuries.
- Onywyn ignores the index. She uses foxglove and night-bloom and wishrose without consulting it. She has not yet harmed anyone.

They are unfailingly polite to each other. Cricket carries weekly letters between them. The letters are mostly about weather and Maud's pies. They have never written about the herbs.

> *"Brother Pell would prefer I sleep at night. I prefer Brother Pell prefer that."* — Mother Onywyn

## NPC interconnections (gossip lines — for codex flavor)

- **Eldra** had tea with Maud every Threnday for thirty-one years.
- **Cricket** is sixteen, an orphan, lives in a loft above the cooperage. **Sir Withering** taught him to read after the previous letter-carrier died.
- **Hod**'s late wife Bramwen was Quill's aunt. Quill was raised partly at the smithy after Bramwen passed. This is why Hod is gentler with Quill than with anyone else and pretends not to be.
- **The Cooper sisters** (Marra and Wisp) at Coopers' Hold are first cousins of Maud's late husband. Maud takes the bank fee personally and only deposits coppers in protest.
- **Pell** married Eldra and Cricket each their own copy of the village psalter for their birthdays. Onywyn was not invited to the cloister for either occasion.

## The Calendar (4 festivals)

| Name | When | What |
|---|---|---|
| **The Quickening** | First week of First-Spring | Bramble-imps grow bold; combat-heavy week. Pell rings the cloister bell at sundown, seven nights. |
| **Lampwright's Eve** | Three nights mid-Last-Spring | Eldra's lanterns are lit at dusk village-wide. The night sky is *deliberately dark* between lit lanterns. Children stay home. |
| **Pieday** | Last Sevenday of High-Summer | Maud's pie contest at the well. Hod judges. Withering brings a cake every year and is gently disqualified. |
| **Last Light** | Winter solstice | The whole village gathers at Old Mother Well after dusk. Each family lights a candle. No one speaks until the candles burn down. Onywyn's bramble-mother once lit hers from inside the hedge. |

## Whispered legends (discovery hooks)

These are folk tales the villagers tell each other. None are confirmed; some are clearly false. Use them in codex landmark entries to build "discovery" feel.

- **The Man at the Bottom of Old Mother Well.** Children claim there's a man asleep down there, breathing. Adults laugh. (There isn't. The village laughs because there is.)
- **The Seventh Cottage.** Old Wagon Road has a bend where you can see *seven* cottages along it. Walk it and count: there are six. Always six. Some say the seventh is for whoever was supposed to come and didn't.
- **The Bramble Bargain.** Onywyn the elder did not bind herself for nothing. She bargained with the hedge: my thinking, slower than yours, in exchange for *one winter*. The winter was bought; the rest is what we live in. (Mother Onywyn does not confirm this. She does not deny it either.)
- **Sallow's Tide.** The shipwreck at Sallow's End was killed by a tide that didn't happen. The tide-tables for that night are clean. The wreck is wedged into the cliff at an angle no real wave makes. Nobody has been down to it in fifty years.
- **Linnet's Vow.** Sir Withering's falcon is named Linnet. The villagers know there used to be *another* Linnet — a person, not a bird. Withering says the bird's name first. They are kind enough not to ask.

## The Wolds beyond Bramblewood (places named, not visited)

These exist in dialog and in items but the player doesn't go to them. Keep that intentional — the world is implied by an edge, not mapped.

- **Trelliswick** — Withering's homeland. North-east, four-day ride. He won't talk about it.
- **Sallow's End** — the tidal flats south-west, three hours' walk. The shipwreck. The cousins.
- **Hag's Furrow** — a wild hedge-line east of the village where Onywyn forages. Cricket won't deliver there.
- **The Marl** — the marshy basin at the far end of Cricket's letter route. Letters to The Marl take three days; he hates the route; he goes anyway.
- **Coopers' Hold** *(the place, not the bank)* — the original Cooper family farm, abandoned. The bank uses the family name out of respect.
- **The Pale Veins** *(dungeon scope: delve)* — Hod's ore quarries. Underground passages that branch into other things. He stops his stories halfway through.

## Anti-anti-patterns (what this lore explicitly *doesn't* do)

- **Doesn't explain the bramble.** Where it came from, what it is, why it grows here and not elsewhere — left undefined. Players who want lore can read the Bramble Bargain and decide for themselves.
- **Doesn't escalate.** The Hedgemother is the largest threat in the world. There is no Worse Bramble. There is no King of Brambles.
- **Doesn't bind the player to the lore.** Player can ignore everything in this file and play the mechanical game cleanly. Lore is reward for curious players, not gating for goal-driven ones.
- **Doesn't lock the calendar to real-world dates.** "First-Spring" / "Last-Spring" / "High-Summer" stay vague. The day-night cycle in code is mechanical (10 minutes per game-day); the calendar is narrative (whenever the player encounters it).
